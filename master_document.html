Peer Support Programmes

Source file: peer_support_programs.md

Last verified: 14 June 2026

These programmes are different from professional counselling. Availability, matching and recruitment windows can change during the academic year.

1. NUS Peer Student Supporters (PSS)

Program Name: NUS Peer Student Supporters
What it is: Trained student volunteers who offer a listening ear and connect peers with appropriate university resources. They are supporters, not counsellors or crisis responders.
Who it's for: NUS students who want to speak with another student about university life, stress, adjustment or feeling alone.
Commitment: A conversation can be a one-off; follow-up depends on what both students arrange.
How to sign up: Open the NUS Peer Student Supporters group through NUSync or use the contact route in the NUS Student Support Directory.
What to expect: A trained peer can listen and help identify next steps. For urgent risk, use crisis_contacts.md instead.
Source: NUSync events directory

2. Peer Connectors Network

Program Name: Peer Connectors Network
What it is: Current NUS students help international students ease into campus life, including through pre-arrival and orientation activities.
Who it's for: New international students, exchange students and students seeking cross-cultural connection.
Commitment: Programme and event based; no general monthly commitment is published.
How to sign up: Watch the NUS International Student Experience page and its orientation links.
What to expect: Practical campus guidance, orientation activities and contact with current students; this is not therapy or emergency care.
Source: NUS International Student Experience

3. International Students Orientation and Pre-Arrival Connection

Program Name: International Students Orientation / Pre-Arrival Connection
What it is: ISCO activities that help incoming international students understand NUS and Singapore and begin meeting peers.
Who it's for: Incoming undergraduate, graduate, exchange and other non-local students, subject to each event's eligibility.
Commitment: Usually a short orientation or event series.
How to sign up: Use the current orientation link on the International Student Experience page.
What to expect: Structured activities and practical information. Peer Connectors may be present at selected events.
Source: NUS OSA International Students

4. Residential Peer Mentorship and Proactive Pastoral Care

Program Name: Residential Peer Mentorship / Proactive Pastoral Care
What it is: NUS Houses use a Proactive Pastoral Care system in which residents are assigned to small support groups. Residential communities also have Peer Mentors, Resident Assistants or Cluster Leaders.
Who it's for: Students living in the relevant House, hall, residential college or student residence.
Commitment: Informal check-ins and residential activities; the exact model differs by residence.
How to sign up: Ask your residence office, Resident Assistant, Peer Mentor or Cluster Leader.
What to expect: A nearby point of contact and invitations to community activities. Residential staff can direct you to professional services when needed.
Source: NUS OSA accommodation overview

5. Residence Community Activities

Program Name: Hall, House, Residential College and Student Residence Activities
What it is: Resident-led programmes across wellness, sports, culture, community service and informal social life.
Who it's for: Residents of the organising hostel; some events may admit guests.
Commitment: Flexible in Student Residences; halls and colleges may also offer higher-commitment teams and committees.
How to sign up: Follow your residence's official channels and ask your Resident Assistant or student committee for the next low-commitment event.
What to expect: Repeated contact with people living nearby, often around a shared activity rather than a formal support conversation.
Source: NUS OSA accommodation overview

6. UCS Group Therapy

Program Name: University Counselling Services Group Therapy
What it is: Clinician-facilitated groups that provide community, shared learning and coping skills. This is professional mental-health support rather than a student-run listening circle.
Who it's for: Full-time NUS students who meet the eligibility criteria for the offered group.
Commitment: Multiple facilitated sessions; the schedule depends on the group.
How to sign up: Review current offerings on the UCS page and follow its registration instructions.
What to expect: A confidential, structured group led by a UCS clinician. UCS states that eligible group therapy is free for full-time NUS students.
Source: University Counselling Services

7. Student Support Managers

Program Name: NUS Student Support Managers
What it is: University staff who can support students facing personal, academic, family or adjustment difficulties and connect them with the right services.
Who it's for: NUS students who are unsure where to start or whose situation crosses several areas of university life.
Commitment: Based on need; it is not a fixed buddy programme.
How to sign up: Use the Student Support Manager link from NUS Health & Wellbeing resources or the Student Support Directory.
What to expect: Practical navigation and support planning, with referral to specialist help where appropriate.
Source: NUS Health & Wellbeing

Wellness Services

Source file: wellness_services.md

Last verified: 14 June 2026

These services handle personal information under their own confidentiality and privacy rules. NUS University Counselling Services states that counselling is confidential, with exceptions including imminent threats to life, legal requirements, consent, or university-referred assessments. Do not assume that every service is anonymous or that no administrative record is created; ask the service if you are concerned about privacy. Seeking support is not an academic grade or disciplinary matter.

1. University Counselling Services (UCS)

Service: University Counselling Services
What it offers: Free counselling for full-time NUS students. Students can discuss stress, loneliness, adjustment, relationships, loss or other concerns; support is not limited to a crisis. UCS also offers eligible group therapy.
How to access: Submit the online appointment request or email UCS@nus.edu.sg for general enquiries.
Walk-in support: Same-day walk-in appointments at Kent Ridge are for urgent emotional support and risk assessment. Last walk-in is 5:30pm Monday-Thursday and 5pm Friday; operating hours and holiday closures apply. UTown and Bukit Timah services are by appointment only.
Response time: NUS does not publish a guaranteed appointment response time. Referrals do not expedite waiting time.
Important: You do not need to be in crisis to request counselling. For immediate psychological support, call Lifeline NUS at +65 6516 7777.
Source: University Counselling Services

2. University Health Centre (UHC)

Service: University Health Centre
What it offers: Primary care, acute care, pharmacy, vaccination and other student health services. Physical symptoms, sleep, appetite and stress can overlap, so medical care may be a useful starting point.
How to access: Visit 20 Lower Kent Ridge Road, Level 1, or call +65 6601 5035. Email uhc_health@nus.edu.sg.
Registration hours: Monday-Wednesday and Friday, 8:30am-12pm and 1:30pm-5pm; Thursday, 8:30am-12pm and 1:30pm-4:30pm. Closed weekends and public/university holidays. Confirm before travelling.
Response time: Depends on demand and the service needed. Telemedicine was listed as temporarily suspended when verified.
Important: For a medical emergency, call 995 or attend an emergency department; UHC directs emergencies to the National University Hospital A&E when it is closed.
Source: NUS University Health Centre

3. Faith, Pastoral and Interfaith Support

Service: NUS faith communities and NUS Interfaith
What it offers: Community, reflective activities, religious fellowship and cross-belief dialogue. Listed communities include Buddhist, Catholic, Christian, Hindu, Muslim, Sikh and interfaith groups. These groups are not substitutes for clinical care.
How to access: Use the NUS OSA Communities and Religion page and contact the relevant group through NUSync. NUS Interfaith announcements are available through Telegram.
Drop-in hours: Not centrally published; ask the group about open events or pastoral conversations.
Response time: Varies by volunteer or group. Use UCS or Lifeline NUS for time-sensitive mental-health needs.
Important: You can approach NUS Interfaith whether you are religious or non-religious.
Source: NUS OSA Communities and Religion

4. NUS Peer Student Supporters

Service: NUS Peer Student Supporters
What it offers: Trained students who listen, provide basic peer support and link students to appropriate resources. They are not mental-health professionals.
How to access: Find NUS Peer Student Supporters through NUSync or the NUS Student Support Directory.
Drop-in hours: No central public drop-in schedule was found; use the current contact channel.
Response time: Not guaranteed.
Important: Use a professional or emergency service when there is immediate risk, severe panic, violence or suicidal intent.
Source: NUSync events directory

5. Student Accessibility Unit (SAU)

Service: Student Accessibility Unit
What it offers: Academic access arrangements, classroom support, learning services, funded psychological assessments, assistive technology, personalised orientation, accessible transport and links to mental-wellbeing support.
How to access: Email accessibility@nus.edu.sg and review Accessibility Support.
Drop-in hours: No public drop-in hours are listed. Contact SAU to discuss documentation and suitable support.
Response time: Not publicly guaranteed. Start early when requesting classroom or examination arrangements.
Important: Students with a diagnosed or suspected condition, including a condition that substantially affects social or academic participation, can ask SAU what evidence and support options apply. Do not assume a diagnosis automatically qualifies for a particular arrangement.
Source: NUS OSA Accessibility Support

6. NUS Office of Financial Aid

Service: Office of Financial Aid
What it offers: Information and applications for NUS-administered financial-aid schemes. Financial strain can worsen isolation, housing difficulty and mental stress, so it is reasonable to seek financial and emotional support at the same time.
How to access: Start at the NUS Financial Aid portal and use its enquiry route for your student category and scheme.
Drop-in hours: Check the current portal or appointment instructions; no universal drop-in arrangement is stated here.
Response time: Scheme and application-cycle dependent. Apply by the published deadline and raise urgent hardship through the official enquiry route.
Important: Financial support is separate from counselling. If money worries are affecting sleep, mood or safety, contact UCS as well.
Source: NUS Office of Admissions - Financial Aid

Hi! I saw that [Club Name] is open to new members. I'm a [Year] student studying [Course], and I'd love to come along to one of your sessions to see what it is like. Is that okay? I'm free on [Day]. Thanks!

Template 2 - Reaching out to a peer mentor

Template 3 - Joining a group chat or society

Template 4 - Low-commitment request for an anxious student

Hi, I'm interested in [Club/Event], but I'm not sure whether it is for me yet. Would it be okay for me to observe or try one session before deciding? I may be quiet at first, but I'd like to learn more.

Hey [Name], I sit near you in [Class/Module]. Would you want to study together sometime? I usually go to [Library/Cafe] on [Day] if you're around. No worries if this week is busy.

Hi! I'm an international student from [Country], and I just found your society or community page. I'd love to meet people and learn more about the community here. When is your next open event?

Hi! Just following up on my message from [Day]. I'm still interested in [Club/Programme]. Is there a current NUSync page, Telegram channel or upcoming session I should use instead? Thanks!

7-Day Belonging Plan Template

Source file: 7day_plan_template.md

Personalised by Belonging Buddy
Last verified: 14 June 2026

Goal: Take one small step every two or three days. There is no pressure to do everything.

Day 1 - Explore (10 minutes)

Browse campus_clubs.md, cultural_societies.md or campus_events_calendar.md.

Two options that look interesting:

  1. [Name]
  2. [Name]

No action is needed yet. Just look.

Day 2 - Rest

Notice whether anything from Day 1 stayed in your mind.

What I noticed: [Write one sentence, or leave blank.]

Day 3 - Reach out (5 minutes)

Send one message using first_message_templates.md.

Who I will message: [Club, programme or person]
Which template I will use: [1-6]

One message. That is enough.

Day 4 - Rest

You do not need to follow up today. A slow reply is not a rejection.

Day 5 - Show up (1-2 hours)

Attend one open event, trial or drop-in session after confirming the details.

Event or session: [Name]
Time and place: [Details]
My exit plan: [For example: stay for 30 minutes, then decide whether to remain.]

You can leave early. You do not have to talk to everyone.

Day 6 - Reflect (5 minutes)

Ask yourself:

  • Was there one person or moment that felt okay?
  • Was the activity itself enjoyable?
  • Would a second visit feel easier, or would another option suit me better?

One thing I noticed: [Write here.]

Even one neutral or comfortable moment is enough.

Day 7 - Choose the next small step

My next step: [Return once / message one person / try a different event / ask for support]
When: [Date or day]

Tell Belonging Buddy how it went so the next plan can be adjusted.

Remember

  • Belonging takes time. One week is a start, not a finish.
  • Feeling awkward is normal and does not mean the event went badly.
  • You are allowed to choose quiet events, leave early and try a different group.
  • For non-urgent support, see wellness_services.md.
  • For urgent emotional support, call Lifeline NUS at +65 6516 7777 or SOS at 1767. If there is immediate danger, call 995 or 999. See crisis_contacts.md.
